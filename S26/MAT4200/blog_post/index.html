Term paper (blog post)

From weeks 8–13, you will be working on a short paper on a topic of your choosing. The papers are intended to be open-ended explorations related to any ideas from the course that you were curious to learn more about. You will be in a group of classmates writing on the same (or similar) topics, but you will write independently. You will post your writing (pseudonymously, if requested) on a class blog.

You will be guided through the blog post in each of your remaining homework assignments.

Timeline

Week 8. Pick group and (tentative) topic.

Week 9. Schedule 5–10 minute group meeting with professor to discuss topic. (Six groups can schedule this before or after class or during the break.)

Week 10. Submit outline and draft paragraphs.

Week 11. Submit progress report of draft and illustration.

Week 12. Submit draft blog post in markdown, and start planning final project video.

Week 13. Submit final blog post, and have outline for final group project video.

Guidelines

Your blog post must include an artifact that you generate, which can be a photo or video of something you made, a SVG you designed, a 3D model that you embed via Google model-viewer, or something in the same spirit.

You cannot use AI tools to generate any part of the text in your draft blog posts. On your final draft only, you can use a tool like Grammarly to check grammar, etc.

Throughout this project, you will talk to at least six people—which includes me and the two students in your group—about the project and ask them a question. The question can be about helping you understand an idea or it can be asking someone to tell you if they understand your explanation.

Your blog post should be between 1000 and 2000 words. (For reference, this document is roughly 1000 words.) Your target audience is your classmates at the start of week 8.

You will write your blog post in markdown with MathJax (or in HTML) and I will host it publicly on my website. You are permitted to use a pseudonym on request.

Possible topics

The following is a list of possible topics you could explore. You are encouraged to choose a topic that is not on this list.

Making Differential Geometry

Make something on the laser cutter, 3D printer, embroidery machine, vinyl cutter, and/or another physical technology that uses the ideas from this class or illustrates ideas from this class. Explain the design process, the mathematical ideas you used, and any trade-offs you had to make along the way.

Pasta by Design

Borrow the book Pasta by Design by George L. Legendre from me or via an interlibrary loan. Implement some of the pasta designs in that book, but also derive some of your own. Describe in detail how you modeled your shapes, what went wrong, and the tradeoffs you encountered.

Applications to physics and engineering

One big motivation for developing differential geometry was because of its physics applications. Describe an application to physics or another science/engineering discipline that you haven't seen in another class (or which you have seen, but want to understand more deeply.) In the first part of your blog post, explain the application to someone in the class. In the second part, explain the application to someone in that major.

Oscilloscope music

In HW3, Choice Exercise 7, I presented you with a video about oscilloscope music. Design some oscilloscope music, run it through an actual oscilloscope, and report how the reality compares to your expectations. (Take lots of photos and videos!)

Mathematical visualization

Describe a set of objects you would like to be able to describe and model digitally. Work in Mathematica, OpenSCAD, Blender, or other programs to illustrate these ideas. Making 3D objects that you can embed in your blog post is a bonus!

More differential geometry

There are ideas from Differential Geometry that we haven't gotten to yet, and still more that we won't get to this term. Some might be hard to explore because they have lots of pre-requisites, but others are within reach. One approach is to take a textbook and work through the preface or first section, and describe the ideas in your own words.

Design homework problems

Throughout this term, I've tried to write a variety of rich homework problems. Now's your turn! Try to write a few multipart homework problems that I could give the next time I teach this course, complete with graphics, 3D models, etc. Begin with a preface where you describe the ideas and motivation and include an answer key at the end.

[Blank], Intuitively

Some of my favorite Math Stack Exchange questions ask the question What's an intuitive way to think about [blank]? Choose a topic that is related to something that we did in the class, and explain it intuitively, with examples, illustrations, photographs, etc.

Different ways of seeing

On HW2, choice exercise 4, I had you compute the volume of a tetrahedron in four different ways. Choose some other idea that you can look at from three or more different perspectives, and show what each perspective has to teach.

Dig into a homework problem

Take a rich homework problem and dig in or generalize it. Examples of problems that could be a good starting point are HW2.8, HW3.5, HW3.8, HW 4.4.4, or HW 4.9.

Higher dimensions

We've given lots of examples of integrals in \(\mathbb R^2\) and \(\mathbb R^3\). How might you describe integrals of interest in higher dimensional spaces? For instance, can you use this to explain the ways in which higher dimensional balls and spheres are similar to or different from our intuition in \(\mathbb R^2\) and \(\mathbb R^3\)?

Seven-dimensional cross product

There is an idea that the cross product only exists in \(\mathbb R^3\) and \(\mathbb R^7\). Explore what this means, why the cross product fails to exist in other dimensions, and other notions that are similar to a cross product.

Make it rigorous

We have talked about many ideas in this class informally. Prove a theorem that we didn't prove in class, or give a rigorous explanation for a definition we used.

Non-orientable surfaces

Many theorems in differential geometry require our regions to be "nice" somehow. Explore what can go wrong when the regions fail to satisfy these definitions. One way things can go wrong is when our regions are not orientable as in the Möbius strip and Klein bottle.

Modern differential geometry

Give an introduction to modern differential geometry by defining and giving examples of manifolds, tangent spaces, and differential forms. If you're feeling bold, you can alternatively try to "Explain Like I'm a Freshman" De Rham cohomology, which in some sense allows us to measure the "$n$-dimensional holes" in a surface or manifold.
