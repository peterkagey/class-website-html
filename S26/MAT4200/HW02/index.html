Exercise 3 [7]
Recall that we have been modeling my Christmas tree in class, which we've been describing as a cone that is $7$ feet tall and $4$ feet in diameter at the base. In particular, we used the parameterization \[ \mathbf{c}(\theta, h) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{2}{7}(7-h) \cos (\theta ) \\ \frac{2}{7}(7-h) \sin (\theta ) \\ h \end{pmatrix},\,\theta \in [0, 2\pi],\,h \in [0,7] \]
  1. Plot the tree in Mathematica with the following code, noting that I have renamed $\theta$ to $t$. 
    ParametricPlot3D[{2/7 (7 - h) Cos[t], 2/7 (7 - h) Sin[t], h}, {t, 0, 2 Pi}, {h, 0, 7}, PlotStyle -> Green]
    Save the image as a PNG, and include it in your write-up.
  2. Now we're going to add some ornaments to the tree. Save your plot of the tree as a variable by prepending treePlot = before ParametricPlot3D. Next we're going to make some ornaments named ornament1, ornament2, etc. to place on the tree. To make the ornament, use code like this 
    ornament1 = Graphics3D[{Red, Sphere[{1, 0, 3.5}, 0.2]}];
    which makes a red ornament at $(1,0,3.5)$ with a radius of $0.2$. Make a few more ornaments with different colors, positions, and radii, and place them on the tree with 
    Show[treePlot, ornament1, ..., ornament4]
    Save the image as a PNG, and include it in your write-up.
  3. By hand or with Mathematica, set up an integral to compute the surface area of the tree. Evaluate the integral by hand with a computer and report your answer as a decimal. (In Mathematica you can numerically integrate a function with NIntegrate.)
  4. Since I can't see ornaments at the back of my tree, I only place the ugly ones back there, and I have a higher density of ornaments at the front. We will model the density of ornaments by $4\frac{(\theta - \pi)^2}{\pi^2}$ ornaments per square foot, where $\theta$ is the angle from the front of the tree.
    1. "The zeroth lesson in mathematics is you should always begin by guessing!"1 Imagine a tree of the size described, and then imagine four ornaments per square foot at the front. What would you guess is the number of ornaments on the tree?
    2. Set up an integral to compute the number of ornaments on the tree according to this model. Could this possibly be the number of ornaments I actually had on my tree?
    3. How many ornaments do I have in the $90^\circ$ wedge centered at the front of the tree versus the $90^\circ$ wedge centered at the back?
  5. Now it's time to string the lights spiraling around the tree! If I want the lights spaced six inches apart between every full turn, what's a parametric equation for the lights? Plot this on the tree as a parametric curve with ParametricPlot3D and include an image.
  6. What is the length of the spiral of lights?

Choice Exercise 4 [≤8]

In this exercise, you are going to compute the volume of a regular unit tetrahedron in four different ways. You can do any subset of parts, but you have to do part 1.

  1. Start by guessing what you think the volume will be, and write your guess as a decimal.
  2. [1] Around 225 BCE, Archimedes discovered that a "pyramid" is three times the volume of a cone with the same base and height.
    1. What is the area $A$ of an equilateral triangle with side lengths of $1$?
    2. What is the height $h$ of a unit tetrahedron? (i.e. what is the height of the tetrahedron if you set it down on a table with a triangular face down?)
    3. What is the volume of a triangular prism with base $A$ and height $h$?
    4. What does Archimedes conclude that the volume of a regular tetrahedron is in this case?
  3. [2] Now we compute the volume of a tetrahedron by slicing, taking an integral from $z = 0$ to $z = h$, where $h$ is computed above.
    1. What is the area of the cross-section at the base when $z = 0$? When $z = h$? What's the formula for the area given arbitrary $z \in [0,h]$?
    2. Set up and evaluate the area over the height. (Don't use a computer for this one.) Is this the same as what you got above?
  4. [2] Next we're going to figure out the volume by doing a double-integral over the base. First start by noticing that we place the tetrahedron on the plane, and it up into six regions of equal volume, such that each region is bounded above by a plane. Here's a 3D model where we're looking at the base.
    1. Let's start by describing the base of the tetrahedron, which is the unit triangle in the $xy$-plane. Place a vertex at $(0,0)$ and another at $(1,0)$. Where does the third vertex of the triangle go? Where it the center of the triangle?
    2. Now we're going to cut up the triangle into sixths, as illustrated on the base of the tetrahedron above. Let's choose the sub-triangle consisting of $(0,0)$, $(1/2,0)$ and the center of the triangle. What is the function describing the line segment from the origin to the center, and what is its domain?
    3. Next we will describe the plane (in $\mathbb{R}^3$) above that region of the base. To determine a plane, we just need three points on the plane. Find the "top" of the tetrahedron in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and use this to describe the plane in the form \[ z = Ax + By + C. \]
    4. Now we're ready to set up a double integral to compute the volume of the tetrahedron, which we will do by scaling our region by $6$: \[ \operatorname{Vol}(\mathrm{tet}) = 6\iint_R f(x,y) dA = 6\int_{x = 0}^\square \int_{y = 0}^{y = \square} \square\, dy\, dx. \] Set up and compute the integral by hand.
  5. [3] Lastly, we're compute the volume of the tetrahedra my favorite way using a couple tricks.
    1. If we take the tetrahedron and scale it by a factor of two, then the volume of the scaled tetrahedron is how many times bigger than the unit tetrahedron?
    2. Notice that we cut up the tetrahedron with side-length $2$ into four unit tetrahedra with the center shape left over. What are the side lengths of the center piece? Look up the name of this shape, which we will call $\mathcal{O}$, which is one of the five Platonic solids.
    3. Use the above to parts to determine the ratio of the volume of the unit tetrahedron to the volume of $\mathcal{O}$.
    4. Notice that we can make a parallelepiped out of $\mathcal{O}$ and two unit tetrahedra. And recall that the volume of a parallelepiped corresponds to the determinant of a matrix.
      Set up the corresponding matrix and compute it's determinant. Now use the ideas above to determine the volume of a tetrahedron.
Choice Exercise 5 [3]

Suppose that there is a wire that spirals along a cone of height $2$ and a base of radius $1$ defined by the function $\boldsymbol{g}\colon[0,2] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$, where \[ \boldsymbol{g}(h) = ((h/2)\cos(10\pi h),(h/2)\sin(10\pi h), 2-h). \] (Notice that $h$ is the vertical distance from the top of the cone.)

We make some measurements of the temperature at various places in the room, and produce a quadratic model for the temperature that best fits the data near the track: \[\begin{align*} T(x,y,z) = 2.1x^2 - 3.2xy + 0.3xz -2.4y^2 + 2.0yz \\ + 0.9z^2 + 6.7 x + 3.6 y - 0.55 z + 105.1. \end{align*}\]

  1. Compute the Jacobian matrix $D\boldsymbol{g}(h)$ and evaluate it at $h = 0.5$.
    Compute the Jacobian matrix $DT(x,y,z)$ and evaluate it at $(x,y,z) = \boldsymbol{g}(0.5)$.
  2. Do the matrix multiplication to compute the derivative $(T \circ \boldsymbol{g})'(0.5)$. Write what this means in words.
  3. The temperature is approximately $104.644$ at $h = 0.5$. Use the linear approximation to estimate for which value of $h$ the temperature is $105$.
  4. You could compose the functions together and take the derivative, but you really don't want to do this. Start a stopwatch, stop the moment you have both (a) gotten the hang of this approach and (b) feel a deep love and appreciation for the multivariable chain rule. Look at your stopwatch and record how long this emotional journey took you.

Choice Exercise 8 [2 + 2 + 8 + 10 + 10]

In this exercise, you will estimate $\pi$ using cubic Bézier curve to approximate the unit circular arc in the first quadrant. Recall that in class, we estimated $\pi$ with the quadratic Bézier curve \[ \mathbf{c}(t) = (1-t)^2 \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{pmatrix} + 2t(1-t) \begin{pmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{pmatrix} + t^2 \begin{pmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{pmatrix} \] and we found that \[ \pi \approx 2 \int_{t=0}^1 \sqrt{8 (t-1) t+4}\,dt \]

  1. [2] We'll start by reviewing what we did in class.
    1. Use Mathematica to plot this curve: 
      ParametricPlot[{(1 - t)^2 {1, 0} + 2 t (1 - t) {1, 1} + t^2 {0, 1}}, {t, 0, 1}]
      Write a guess of what you think will happen if you extend the domain from $t \in [0,1]$ to $t \in [-1,2]$. Now edit the code to change the domain and say what actually happened. Right click to save either (or both) plots as an image, and include them in your writeup with the $\LaTeX$ command \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{path/to/image.png}.
    2. Use Mathematica to compute this integral with 
      2*Integrate[Sqrt[4+8(-1+t)t],{t,0,1}]
      Highlight the output, and select Copy As > LaTeX, and paste it here.
    3. Wrap the whole expression in an N[...], to get a numerical approximation of the value. Paste that here too. What estimate of $\pi$ does this give?
  2. [2] Now we'll do the same thing, but with a cubic Bézier curve. Figure out which values of $\ell$ make the curve with control points $\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,0)$, $\mathbf{v}_2 = (1,\ell)$, $\mathbf{v}_3 = (\ell,1)$, and $\mathbf{v}_4 = (0,1)$ go through the point $(\sqrt{2}/2,\sqrt{2}/2)$. Include a plot of the resulting Bézier curve, and state the resulting numerical estimate for $\pi$ that this gives?
  3. [8] The cubic Bézier curve approximation that you found in the above part is actually not the best approximation of the quarter circle in a particular sense. We might say the best approximation of the quarter circle is the one where $|\mathbf{c}(t)|$ is "closest" to $1$, as measured by the min/max: We want to minimize the maximal value of $\big|(|\mathbf{c}(t)|-1)\big|$ over all inputs $t \in [0,1]$. Find the value of $\ell$ that does this, and give it's approximation for $\pi$.
    Is this the same as if we want to minimize the maximal value of $(|\mathbf{c}(t)|-1)^2$?
  4. [10 + 10] You can do the following for credit on future assignments. As far as I know, these are all open questions. However, they are the sorts of questions that are open because nobody has thought to look them yet, not because they are necessarily especially difficult. It would make for a wonderful paper in this class, that might even be publishable in an academic journal. What are the values of $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$ if you want to do the same min/max with control points $\mathbf{v}_1 = (\alpha,0)$, $\mathbf{v}_2 = (\beta,\gamma)$, $\mathbf{v}_3 = (\gamma,\beta)$, and $\mathbf{v}_4 = (0,\alpha)$.
