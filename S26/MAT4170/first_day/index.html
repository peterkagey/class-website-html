First day activity

Exploring symmetry

Today we'll explore the idea of "symmetry," which has a "you know it when you see it" quality that is surprisingly tricky to define.

Introducing symmetry

  1. Is the following symmetric? How many symmetries does the figure have?
  2. How about these? How many symmetries do they have?
  3. Is this figure more symmetric? Less symmetric? The same amount of symmetry? How many symmetries does this have?
  4. How about this one? How many symmetries? Are they the related to the symmetries of either of the previous two?
  5. At this point, try to define what a symmetry is.

Symmetries of physical objects

  1. When we describe the world, there are always details that we ignore. Is a shirt symmetric? Sure, but not really if you choose to include every detail: the placement of the button, or the words on the tag, or the fact that sewing isn't perfect. In the following figures, the amount of symmetry depends on what you decide to include or ignore. Discuss the different kinds of symmetries that you have depending on what you abstract away.
  2. Grab some objects from the front of the room, and try to describe all of their symmetries. Be very specific about what details you're paying attention to (e.g. color, etc.) and what details you're abstracting away (e.g. "ignore the scratch at the top")
  3. When describing the symmetries, you are encouraged to come up with your own notation. You could just give every symmetry a unique name, but it might be more interesting to try to give more descriptive notation. In your notation, do all of the symmetries have a unique name?
  4. Once you've named all of the symmetries perform a symmetry on an object, and see if your group mates agree with the name you gave it. Does the name of a symmetry depend on your perspective?

Coming up with your own objects

  1. Can you draw or describe an object with precisely $3$ symmetries? With precisely $6$?
  2. Draw another object with precisely $3$ symmetries. Are those symmetries somehow the "same" as the symmetries of the other object?
  3. In the first part, there were two objects both with six symmetries. Do you think those symmetries are essentially the same? Or are they different at all? Regardless of how you answer, try to justify your answer.
  4. What if there are two objects in play that we can move around. Does the following (duplicated) figure have more or fewer symmetries than if we just had one?
  5. Can an object have an infinite number of symmetries? If an object has an infinite number of symmetries, can we do "half" a symmetry? Are the multiple ways to have an infinite number of symmetries?
  6. Can the symmetries of one object be a subset of the symmetries of another?

Asking your own questions!

One perhaps understated piece of mathematical maturity is learning to ask your own questions. If you have time left over, ask a few questions about symmetries. Vague questions are okay (nearly all mathematical questions start off vague) but try to make the questions as precise as you can.
