Exercise 2 [7]

There are some ideas from class that in hindsight I wish I had emphasized more. In this exercise, you will self-teach ideas that we glanced over in class.

  1. We defined the dihedral group in terms of its group presentation: \[ D_n = \langle r, f \mid r^n = f^2 = (rf)^2 = e \rangle. \]
    1. Prove that \(r^kf = fr^{n-k}\) for all \(0 < k < n\).
    2. Prove that every element can be written in the form \(r^kf^\ell\) for \(0 \leq k < n\) and \(\ell \in \{0, 1\}\)
    3. Prove that the order (number of elements) of the group is \(D_n = 2n\).
  2. We say that a group \((G,*)\) is an abelian group if and only if \( a * b = b * a \) for all \(a, b \in G\).
    1. Prove that if \(G\) is generated by \(g_1\) and \(g_2\) then \(G\) is abelian if and only if \(g_1 * g_2 = g_2 * g_1\).
    2. Suppose \(G\) is generated by \(g_1, g_2, \dots, g_n\). Do you think that \(G\) is abelian if and only if \(g_i * g_j = g_j * g_i\) for all \(i, j \in \{1, 2, \dots, n\}\)? Explain your reasoning, but you do not have to provide a proof.
    3. Prove that cyclic groups \(C_n\) are abelian.
    4. Prove that the general linear group of \(2 \times 2\) real-valued matrices \[ GL_2(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \mid \det(A) \neq 0\} \] is not abelian.
    5. Prove that the dihedral group \(D_n\) is abelian if and only if \(n \leq 2\).

  3. The direct product of groups is a way of combining two groups into a new group. The notation can look a bit intimidating, but the basic idea is simple: just treat the groups independently. Here's the formal definition, but keep in mind that \(*\), \(\star\), and \(\odot\) are just names for binary operations like \(+\), \(\times\), and \(\circ\).

    Given two groups $(G_1,*)$ and $(G_2,\star)$, we define the direct product of groups as \[ G_1 \times G_2 = \{(g_1, g_2) \mid g_1 \in G_1 \text{ and } g_2 \in G_2\} \] with the binary operation, \(\odot\), where \[ (g_1, g_2)\odot(g'_1,g'_2) = (g_1 * g'_1, g_2 \star g'_2). \]

    1. Let \({(G_1, *) = (D_4, *)}\) be the dihedral group of the square, and let \({(G_2, \star) = (S_3, \circ)}\) be the symmetric group under function composition. Show that in \(G_1 \times G_2\), \[ \left(f, (132) \right) \odot \left(r, (23) \right) = \left(r^3f, (13) \right). \]
    2. Prove that for any two groups $G_1$ and $G_2$, the direct product of groups is a group.
    3. Prove that if $G_1 \times G_2$ is abelian if and only if $G_1$ and $G_2$ are both abelian.
    4. Let $\mathbb{Z}/k\mathbb{Z}$ denote the group of the integers mod $k$ under addition. Let $G = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$. Compute the order of $G$. Prove that $G$ has an element of order $|G|$.
    5. Let $G = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. Compute the order of $G$. Prove the $G$ has no element of order $|G|$.

Choice Exercise 4 [5+≤7] (review/practice)
  1. [5] We sometimes describe groups by their presentation, \(\langle S \mid R \rangle\), where $S$ is a set of generators, and \(R\) is a set of relations. Recall, we define the dihedral group of the \(n\)-gon as \[ D_n = \langle \underbrace{r, f}_S \mid \underbrace{r^n = f^2 = (rf)^2 = e}_R \rangle. \] What this means is that every element of $s \in D_n$ can be written as a word \[ s = g_1^{e_1}g_2^{e_2}\dots g_m^{e_m}, \] where $g_i \in S = \{r, f\}$, $e_i \in \mathbb{Z}$, and where two words are equal if we can modify one to the other using the relations in $R$.
    1. Draw a Cayley diagram of the group with presentation \[ G = \langle r_1, r_2 \mid r_1^2 = r_2^2 = (r_1 r_2)^3 = e \rangle. \] (Hint: \(|G| \leq 10\).)
    2. Use the generators and relations to prove that $r^{-3}frf^3 = r \in D_5$.
    3. Prove that for all positive integers $k$, $(r^kf)^2 = e \in D_n$.
    4. Write a group presentation of the cyclic group $C_n$.
  2. [≤7] Recall that an equivalence relation $\sim$ on a set $X$ is a relation that is
    • Reflexive: \(x \sim x\) for all \(x \in X\).
    • Symmetric: If \(x \sim y\) then \(y \sim x\).
    • Transitive: If \(x \sim y\) and \(y \sim z\), then \(x \sim z\).
    Do any subset of the following.
    1. [2] State which of the following are equivalence relations and briefly describe why.
      1. Let \(X = \mathbb{Z}\) be the set of integers, and the relation be equals \((=)\).
      2. Let \(X = \mathbb{R}\) be the set of real numbers, and the relation be less than or equal to \((\leq)\).
      3. Let \(X = \mathbb{Q}\) be the set of rational numbers, and the relation be less than \((<)\).
      4. Let \(X = D_4\) be the set of symmetries of the square, and the relation be not equal to \((\neq)\).
    2. [1] Suppose we have a function $f \colon X \to Y$ the relation \(R\) is defined as \[ x_1 \mathrel{R} x_2 \Longleftrightarrow f(x_1) = f(x_2). \] Prove or disprove that \(R\) is an equivalence relation.
    3. [1] Consider the subgroup $H \leq (G,*)$ and define the relation \(R\) as \[ a \mathrel{R} b \Longleftrightarrow a^{-1} * b \in H. \] Prove or disprove that \(R\) is an equivalence relation.
    4. [1] Consider the subgroup $H \leq (G,*)$ and define the relation \(R\) as \[ a \mathrel{R} b \Longleftrightarrow a * b \in H. \] Prove or disprove that \(R\) is an equivalence relation.
    5. [2] Let $(G,*)$ be a group and define the relation \(R\) as \[ a \mathrel{R} b \Longleftrightarrow a = g * a * g^{-1} \text{ for some } g \in G. \] Prove or disprove that \(R\) is an equivalence relation.

  2. We begin with the definition of a relation. Given a set $X$ we say a relation $R$ is a subset of the Cartesian product $R \subseteq X \times X$ and we say that $a \mathrel{R} b$ if if $(a,b) \in R$.

    For example, if $X = \{1,2,3\}$ then if $R$ is the "less than" relation, then formally $R = \{(1,2),(1,3),(2,3)\}$.

    Do any subset of the following. You'll get $1$ point if you find at least four, $2$ points if you find at least six, $3$ points if you find exactly seven, and $4$ points if you find all eight.

    1. Reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
    2. Reflexive, symmetric, and not transitive.
    3. Reflexive, not symmetric, and transitive.
    4. Reflexive, not symmetric, and not transitive.
    5. Not reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
    6. Not reflexive, symmetric, and not transitive.
    7. Not reflexive, not symmetric, and transitive.
    8. Not reflexive, not symmetric, and not transitive.
  3. We can embed the dihedral group $D_n$ into $S_n$. (In fact, Cayley's theorem states that we can embed any finite group $G$ into $S_N$ for large enough some $N$.) For example, if we mark the corners of the square $1$, $2$, $3$, and $4$, then rotating/reflecting the square permutes these numbers.

    2. We can also write this in terms of the generators of a subgroup. Find two elements $x, y \in S_4$ such that the eight permutations given above are equal to $\langle x ,y \rangle$.

Choice Exercise 8 [7+6] (challenge)
  1. [7] The group of $n \times n$ real-valued matrices with non-zero determinant under matrix multiplication is called \[ GL_n(\mathbb{R}) = \{A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \mid \det(A) \neq 0\}. \]
    1. Let $GL^+_n(\mathbb{R})$ be the subset of $GL_n(\mathbb{R})$ which consists of matrices with positive determinant. Prove that $GL^+_n(\mathbb{R})$ forms a subgroup.
    2. Prove that $GL^+_n(\mathbb{R})$ is a normal subgroup of \(GL_n(\mathbb{R})\), compute the order of the quotient group \( GL_n(\mathbb{R})/GL^+_n(\mathbb{R}) \), and describe the coset that contains the matrix $-I_n$ (where $I$ is the $n\times n$ identity matrix.)
    3. Let $SL_n(\mathbb{R})$ be the subset of $GL_n(\mathbb{R})$ which consists of matrices with determinant one. Prove that $SL_n(\mathbb{R})$ is a subgroup of $GL_n(\mathbb{R})$.
    4. Prove that \(\{I,-I\} \leq SL_n(\mathbb{R})\), and moreover that \(\{I,-I\} \lhd SL_n(\mathbb{R})\).
    5. Give an example of two matrices that are in the identity coset of the quotient group \( SL_n(\mathbb{R})/\{I,-I\}, \) and give an example of two matrices that are in some other coset.
  2. [6] We say that the orthogonal group \(O(n,\mathbb{R})\) is the set of \(n\times n\) matrices whose columns are orthonormal vectors, that is each column viewed as a vector has magnitude \(1\) and the dot product of any two distinct columns is zero.
    1. Show that a matrix \(A \in O(n,\mathbb{R})\) if and only if $A^T A = AA^T = I_n$.
    2. Show that \(O(n,\mathbb{R})\) is a group for all integers $n \geq 1$.
    3. The special orthogonal group \(SO(n,\mathbb{R})\) is the subset of \(SO(n,\mathbb{R})\) consisting of all matrices whose determinant is positive. Prove that this is a group. (This is the group of rotations of the unit hypersphere in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)).
