Exercise 3 [6]

Suppose that you have two (indistinguishable) coins with the following symmetries: you can flip over the left coin, the right coin, both, or neither, and you can swap their places. Some examples of the states of these coins are HT, TT, HT and so on.

  1. How many different states can the coins be in? How many symmetries are there? (These are equal!)
  2. Draw a Cayley diagram for this group of symmetries. Start by choosing a symmetry and apply it until you get back to where you started. Have you hit all of the possible states?
    If not, choose a new symmetry (and a new color arrow) and apply it to all of the existing states (and apply the previous symmetry to all of your new states). Now have you hit all of the possible states? If not, rinse and repeat.
  3. Redraw the diagram to make it as symmetric as you can. Take a picture or screenshot of this, put it in the same folder as your .tex file, and embed it in the LaTeX with the command \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{path/to/image_file.png}.
    (Note, you will need to save the file as a JPEG or PNG. If you don't know how to do this, ask on Discord.)
  4. Each color of arrow in the diagram corresponds to what we call a generator. How many generators did you use?
  5. Is this group commutative? (i.e. does \(x * y = y * x\) for all \(x\) and \(y\)?)

Exercise 5 [1]

We use lots of symbols to describe the "binary operation" for group elements depending on the context: $\star$, $*$, $\circ$, $+$, $\times$, juxtaposition1—you name it! These symbols have different meanings depending on the context.

  1. Our first example of a binary operation was $*$, which we read as "followed by." If $h$ is a horizontal reflection (i.e. over the $y$-axis) and $r$ is a $120^\circ$ rotation in the positive (counterclockwise) direction. Write in words what symmetry $r * h$ describes.
  2. Just as easily, we could define a binary operation such as $\star$, and declare that our symbol means "after." In this case, we would read the symmetries "right-to-left." Write in words what symmetry $r \star h$ describes.
  3. When $f$ and $g$ are functions, then function composition gives us a way to combine two functions to get a new function. We denote this function as $f \circ g$. Does $\circ$ behave like "followed by" or does it behave like "after"?

Choice Exercise 11 [4]

In class we saw that $D_3$ is not commutative: that is, we can find $x,y \in D_3$ such that $x*y \neq y*x$.

One way of proving that a group is commutative is by proving that its generators are commutative. That is, if you can draw the Cayley diagram with two colors of arrows representing $g_1$ and $g_2$ AND $g_1 * g_2 = g_2 * g_1$, then $x * y = y * x$.

One way of proving that a group is not commutative is by finding a specific example where $x * y \neq y * x$.
