Exercise 3 [6]
  1. Let $(G, *) = (\mathbb{Z}, +)$, and let $a = 1 \in G$ and $b = 3 \in G$. Compute $a^4 * b^{-2} \in \mathbb{Z}$.
    (Notice that superscripts aren't doing the same thing that they do in ordinary arithmetic!)
  2. Let $g \in G$, and let $\langle g \rangle$ be the cyclic subgroup of $G$ generated by $g$. Prove that the order of the element $g$ is equal to the order of the subgroup $\langle g \rangle$.
  3. Consider the cyclic group $C_{20}$, where $r$ is an $18^\circ$ rotation, and thus $|r| = 20$. Find all elements $r^n \in C_{20}$ such that $\langle r^n \rangle = C_{20}$.
  4. Give an example of an infinite group $(G,*)$ and a subset $H \subseteq G$ which is closed under $*$, but where $H$ is not a subgroup of $G$.
  5. Prove that if $a \equiv a' \pmod n$, $b \equiv b' \pmod n$, and $a + b \equiv c \pmod n$, then \[ a' + b' \equiv c \pmod n. \]

Choice Exercise 4 [≤14] (review/practice)
  1. [2] Certain mattress manufacturers recommend flipping a mattress every 6 months to prevent uneven wear, alternating between each of its four symmetries.
    1. Prove that the "mattress flipping" group cannot be generated by a single element, which means that you cannot do the same procedure each six months if you want even wear.
    2. Explain why flipping the mattress over each spring and rotating it $180^\circ$ each fall will hit each of the four symmetries equally.
  2. [2] Prove that if $G$ a group with a subgroup $H \leq G$. Then if $h \in H$ and $g \in G \setminus H$ (i.e. $g$ is in $G$ but not in $H$) then $g * h \notin H$.
  3. [2] Explain why the inverse of the permutation $(12345)(6)(789)$ is $(987)(6)(54321)$. And use this principle find the inverse of $\pi = (726314)(95)$ in cycle notation.
  4. [1] Give an example of three permutations $\pi_1, \pi_2, \pi_3 \in S_9$ that commute with $\sigma = (125)(79)$, and two that don't.
  5. [2] There are two elements in $D_4$ that commute with every other element. In other words, there exist two distinct elements $g_1$ and $g_2$ such that $a * g_1 = g_1 * a$ and $a * g_2 = g_2 * a$ for all $a \in D_4$. Find them.
  6. [2] Prove that if $G$ is a finite group of order $n > 2$, then there are no subgroups of $G$ of order $n - 1$.
  7. [1] Prove that the subgroup relation is transitive: if $H \leq G$ and $K \leq H$, then $K \leq G$.
  8. [1] Give an example of a group $G$ and two elements $a,b \in G$ such that \( (a*b)^2 \neq a^2 * b^2 \).
  9. [1] Find the smallest nonnegative integer $k$ such that $1234 \equiv k \pmod{11}$.

  2. [4] Let $a \in (G,*)$ and recall that we define $\langle a \rangle$ as $\langle a \rangle = \{a^n \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. Recall that we define \[ a^n = \underbrace{a * a * \dots * a}_{n \text{ times}} \qquad \text{and} \qquad a^{-n} = \underbrace{a^{-1} * a^{-1} * \dots * a^{-1}}_{n \text{ times}}. \] We will prove that $a^k * a^m = a^{k + m}$ for all $k, m \in \mathbb{Z}$.
    1. Start by explaining why if $k \geq 0$ and $m \geq 0$, then $a^k * a^m = a^{k + m}$.
    2. Explain why an essentially similar argument shows that if $k \leq 0$ and $m \leq 0$, then $a^k * a^m = a^{k + m}$.
    3. Now, let $n = -k$.
      1. Show that if $m > k$ then $a^m * (a^{-1})^k = a^{m+n}$.
      2. Show that if $m = k$ then $a^m * (a^{-1})^k = a^{m+n}$.
      3. Show that if $m < k$ then $a^m * (a^{-1})^k = a^{m+n}$.
    4. State why the above is done "without loss of generality" with respect to the negative exponent being on the right. In other words, write a sentence stating why the proofs where the inverses are on the left are essentially the same.
    5. An abelian group is a group where $g_1 * g_2 = g_2 * g_1$ for all $g_1, g_2 \in G$. Use the identity $a^k * a^m = a^{k + m}$ to prove that $\langle a \rangle$ is an abelian group.
  3. [2] Let $(G,*)$ be a group and let $a \in G$. Let $H$ be the set of elements that commutes with $a$: \[ H = \{ g \in G \mid g * a = a * g \}. \] Prove or disprove whether $H$ is a subgroup of $G$. (Sometimes $H$ is denoted $C_G(\{a\})$.)
  4. [1] Prove or disprove. If $A$ and $B$ are $n \times n$ matrices, and $AB = A$, then $B$ is the identity matrix, $B = I_n$.

Choice Exercise 8 [4] ⭐️

Here we will study the subgroups of the symmetries of the square, \[ D_4 = \{e, r, r^2, r^3, f, rf, r^2f, r^3f\} \]

  1. Write down the (trivial) subgroup of order $1$ and the (improper) subgroup of order $8$, and say what these mean geometrically.
  2. Write down the five subgroups of order $2$, and describe what they mean geometrically.
  3. Write down the three subgroups of order $4$, and describe in words what they mean geometrically.
  4. What do you notice about the orders of the subgroups?
  5. Are any of these subgroups "essentially similar" to each other? If so, which ones?
