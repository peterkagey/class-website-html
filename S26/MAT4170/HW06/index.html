Exercise 3 [5]

Humans have determined all groups of order \(n < 2^{11}\) where we consider two groups to be equivalent if there is an isomorphism between them. There are $49\,487\,367\,289$ groups of order $2^{10} = 1024$ up to isomorphism, and it is an open question to determine the number of groups of order $2^{11} = 2048$.

Here, you will give examples of each of the groups of order \(n \leq 11\) up to isomorphism. You will need prove that each of the groups you find is not isomorphic to any of the others. It will probably be useful to keep in mind that the direct product $G_1 \times G_2$ is abelian if and only if $G_1$ and $G_2$ is abelian.

Give examples of each of the following:

  1. one group of order $1$;
  2. one group of order $p$ for each prime $p$;
  3. two groups of order $4$ (both abelian);
  4. two groups of order $6$ (one is abelian);
  5. five groups of order $8$ (three are abelian, and one group we saw on Exam 1);
  6. two groups of order $9$ (both are abelian); and
  7. two groups of order $10$ (one is abelian).

Choice Exercise 6 [≤9] 🌟 (Symmetric group practice)

Do any subset of the following for 1 point each:

  1. Let $\pi = (1\ 5\ 3\ 8\ 4\ 6) \in S_9$, prove that $|\pi| = 6,$ where $|\pi|$ is the order of the element $\pi$.
  2. Write in words how you know that if a cycle has length $k$ then it has order $k$.
  3. Prove that $\langle \pi \rangle \in S_9$ is isomorphic to $C_6$.
  4. Let $\pi' = (1\ 5)(3\ 8\ 4) \in S_9$, prove that the order of $\pi' = 6$.
  5. Look up what it means for two cycles to be disjoint. In particular, are $(1\ 5\ 9\ 7)$ and $(3\ 8\ 4)$ disjoint? Are $(1\ 5\ 9\ 8)$ and $(3\ 8\ 4)$ disjoint?
  6. Write in words how you know that $(1\ 5\ 9\ 7)(3\ 8\ 4) = (3\ 8\ 4)(1\ 5\ 9\ 7)$ but $(1\ 5\ 9\ 8)(3\ 8\ 4) \neq (3\ 8\ 4)(1\ 5\ 9\ 8)$.
  7. Write in words how you know that \[ \left((1\ 5\ 9\ 7)(3\ 8\ 4)\right)^7 = (1\ 5\ 9\ 7)^7(3\ 8\ 4)^7 = (1\ 5\ 9\ 7)^{-1}(3\ 8\ 4)^1 = (1\ 7\ 9\ 5)(3\ 8\ 4). \]
  8. Suppose that we write $\sigma = c_1 c_2 \dots c_n$ as a product of disjoint cycles. Write $\pi'$ (defined above) in this form.
  9. Suppose that we write $\sigma = c_1 c_2 \dots c_n$ as a product of disjoint cycles. Prove that the order of sigma $|\sigma| \leq \operatorname{LCM}(|c_1|,|c_2|,\dots,|c_n|)$, where $|c_i|$ is the order (equivalently length) of the cycle.
  10. Give an example of a $3$-cycle and a $4$-cycle whose product is a $2$-cycle.

Choice Exercise 7 [≤10] 🌟 (True/false)

Attempt the following True/False questions and give explanations for your reasoning, then open the "True/False answers" at the end of the problem and check your answers. If you got the answer or explanation wrong, write the correct answer here and say something about why you got it wrong originally.

  1. Let $G$ be a group with order $n$, and denote the order of an element $g \in G$ by $|g|$. Then the order of $G$ is equal to \[ \gcd(|g_1|, |g_2|, \dots, |g_n|) = n. \]
  2. If $\phi\colon G \to H$ is a homomorphism that is 1-to-1, then $\phi\colon G \to \operatorname{Im}(\phi)$ is a isomorphism.
  3. If $\phi\colon G \to H$ is a homomorphism that is onto, then $\operatorname{Im}(\phi) \triangleleft H$.
  4. If $G$ is a non-abelian group and $H$ is abelian, then the only homomorphism $\phi \colon G \to H$ is the trivial homomorphism defined by $\phi(g) = e_H$ for all $g \in G$.
  5. If $\varphi \colon G \to H$ is an isomorphism, then there always exists an isomorphism \({\psi \colon H \to G}\).
  6. Let $G \times H$ be a direct product of groups. Then the set \[ N = \{(g, e_H) \mid g \in G\} \subseteq G \times H \] is a normal subgroup of $G \times H$.
  7. If $G/N \cong H$ then $G \cong N \times H$.
  8. The map $\varphi\colon \mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z} \to C_{16}$ given by $\varphi(1) = r \in C_{16}$ is a homomorphism.
  9. There is a homomorphism $\psi\colon D_6 \to C_6$ such that $\psi(r) = r \in C_6$ and $\psi(f) = e \in C_6$.
  10. There is a homomorphism $\phi\colon C_6 \to D_6$ such that $\phi(r) = r \in D_6$.

True/False answers
  1. False. Consider $G = C_2 \times C_2$.
  2. True. By definition, every map is (surjective) onto it's image, so this map is a bijective homomorphism.
  3. True. If it's onto, then $\operatorname{Im}(\phi) = H$, and $H \triangleleft H$.
  4. False. Let $G = D_3$ and $H = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. Then the map which sends $f \to 1$ and $r \to 0$ is onto but not the trivial map.
  5. True. The inverse map $\psi = \varphi^{-1}$ exists (because it's an bijection) and it is structure preserving. (See Exercise 7.1.)
  6. True. For all $g_0 \in G$ and $h \in H$, $(g_0, h) * (g, e_H) * (g_0, h)^{-1} = (g_0gg_0^{-1}, e_H) \in N$.
  7. False. Use the example above in part 4. The kernel is $\langle r \rangle = \{e, r, r^2\}$, and the image is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, but $\langle r \rangle \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ is abelian, so it's not isomorphic to $D_3$.
  8. False. $e = \varphi(0) = \varphi(4 + 4) \neq \varphi(4) * \varphi(4) = r^4 * r^4 = r^8$.
  9. False. Notice that $rf = fr^5$, but $\psi(rf) = \psi(r) * \psi(f) = r * e = r$, but $\psi(fr^5) = \psi(f) * \psi(r)^5 = r^5$.
  10. True. $C_6 = \langle r_c \mid r_c^6 \rangle$, and the map is structure preserving because $\phi(r_c^6) = r_d^6 = e \in D_6$.
Choice Exercise 8 [≤13 + 5] 🌟 (challenge + programming)

Suppose that you have three (indistinguishable) coins, and you can flip over any subset of them and you can permute their places. Some examples of the states of these coins are HTT, THT, THT and so on. Let's call this group \(G\). We're going to show that this is related to the (Coxeter) group \(B_3\) of symmetries of the cube \[ B_3 = \langle r_1, r_2, r_3 \mid r_1^2 = r_2^2 = r_3^2 = (r_1r_2)^4 = (r_2r_3)^3 = (r_1r_3)^2 = e\rangle, \] where \(B_3\) consists of the \(48\) symmetries of the cube (including reflections).

  1. [3] Show that \(G\) can be generated by the following three elements:
    1. \(x\), which is flipping over the left coin,
    2. \(s_1\), which is swapping the left and middle coins, and
    3. \(s_2\), which is swapping the middle and right coins.
  2. [2] Show that there is a homomorphism \(\varphi\colon B_3 \to G\) with \(\varphi(r_1) = x\), \(\varphi(r_2) = s_1\), and \(\varphi(r_3) = s_2\).
  3. [1] How many ways are there to choose a face of the cube, followed by an edge on that face, followed by a vertex on that edge? (This structure of \(v \subset e \subset f\) is called a "flag." And because the cube is "flag transitive," the number of flags is equal to the number of symmetries.)
  4. [1] Prove that the homomorphism \(\varphi\colon B_3 \to G\) is an isomorphism.
  5. [2] Here's the generalization to the symmetries of the \(n\)-dimensional hypercube: \[ B_n = \langle r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n \mid r_i^2 = (r_1r_2)^4 = (r_jr_{j+1})^3 = (r_kr_\ell)^2 = e, \text{ for } 1 < j < n \text{ and } |k - \ell| \geq 2 \rangle. \] In this case, we're dealing with \(n\) coins, and the homomorphism \(\varphi\colon G \to B_n\) sends
    • "flip over the leftmost coin" to \(r_1\) and
    • "swap coins \(k-1\) and \(k\)" to \(r_k\).
    Show that \(B_2\) is isomorphic to \(D_4\), the dihedral group of the square.
  6. [1] Spend five minutes reading about or watching a YouTube video about "Coxeter groups," and say what you learned or are curious about.
  7. [3] Prove that there is a homomorphism $\psi\colon B_3 \to GL_3(\mathbb{R})$ (the codomain is $3 \times 3$ real-valued matrices) which sends \[ \begin{align*} \psi(r_1) &= \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1\end{pmatrix} \\ \psi(r_2) &= \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0\end{pmatrix} \\ \psi(r_3) &= \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix} \end{align*} \]
  8. [5] You want a programming problem? Here you go!
    1. Generate all of the $48$ words $r_{i_1}r_{i_2} \cdots r_{i_m}$, such that no two words correspond to the same symmetry of $B_3$.
    2. In Mathematica (or another program) write a function that takes in a point $(x,y,z)$ and outputs all $48$ points in the set \[ \{A \cdot (x,y,z) \mid A \in \operatorname{Im}(\psi)\}, \] where $\psi$ is the homomorphism above. Then feed these $48$ points to ConvexHullRegion to get a polyhedron with octahedral symmetry. Put in various points, and include images of at least three interesting shapes that result from this.
