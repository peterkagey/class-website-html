Choice Exercise 7 [3]

In abstract algebra, we often study various "binary operations" that take two elements from a set and combine them to get another element of a set.

Examples of this are:

  • integers under addition $$+ \colon \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}$$ (add two integers and get another integer)
  • square matrices under matrix multiplication $$* \colon \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2} \times \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2} \to \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 2}$$ (matrix multiply two square matrices and get another square matrix)
  • polynomials under function composition $$\circ \colon \mathbb{R}[x] \times \mathbb{R}[x] \to \mathbb{R}[x]$$ (if $f(x)$ is a polynomial and $g(x)$ is a polynomial, then $f(g(x))$ is a polynomial)

Prove that if $\star\colon X \times X \to X$ is a binary operation and $a \star b = b \star a$ for all $a, b \in X$, then $$a_1 \star a_2 \star \dots \star a_n = a_n \star \dots \star a_2 \star a_1$$ for all positive integers $n \in \mathbb{Z}.$ (A technicality is that you'll need to assume that the binary operation is associative, which just means that while you can't rearrange the terms, you "collapse" the values with $\star$ in any order you'd like.)

Choice Exercise 10 [1 + ‚â§3 + 4]

When I was a senior in college, I contributed my first sequence to the On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences (OEIS). I submitted my second contribution, sequence number A248122, a few months after I graduated. For the second sequence, I used recursion to count the "number of strings of length $n$ over a three-letter alphabet that begin with a nontrivial palindrome."

A three-letter alphabet $\{a, b, c\}$ just means that we only have three letters to use. A palindrome is a sequence of letters that is the same forwards and backwards, such as $aa,$ or $abba,$ or $caabaac.$ A non-trivial palindrome just means that we don't count one-letter words ($a$ or $b$ or $c$) as palindromes, even though they are the same forwards and backwards.

  1. [1] Give examples of the following:
    • All $5$ length-$3$ words that begin with $a$ and begin with a non-trivial palindrome.
    • All $4$ length-$3$ words that begin with $a$ but do not begin with a non-trivial palindrome.
    • A $7$-letter word that does not begin with a non-trivial palindrome.
    • A non-trivial palindrome at the beginning of $abbccaccbbacbacbccbcc$.
  2. [‚â§3] Perhaps my first original proof was that the number of such words of length $n$ was given by the formula $$\begin{align*}a(0) &= a(1) = 0, \text{ and}\\ a(n) &= 3a(n-1) + 3^{\lceil n/2 \rceil} - a(\lceil n/2 \rceil)\text{ for } n \geq 2 \text{,} \end{align*}$$ where if $x \in \mathbb{R}$ then the ceiling of $x$ is written $\lceil x \rceil$ and is the least integer greater than or equal to $x$. (For example, $\lceil 7.26 \rceil = 8$ and $\lceil 9 \rceil = 9$.)
    • Compute (by hand) $\lceil 3.1415926 \rceil$, $\lceil 3100 \rceil$, and $\lceil -9.19 \rceil$.
    • Start a stopwatch, and try to write a computer program that will compute $a(26) = 1833980928771.$ You get $n$ points for each $\lceil n/9 \rceil$ points you spend on the problem, with a maximum of three points.
  3. [4] Recursion and induction are essentially the same idea. Use induction to prove that the formula is correct.
