Required Exercise 2 [3]

Proposition. A function $f \colon A \to B$ is a bijection if and only if there exists a function $g \colon B \to A$ such that $g(f(a)) = a$ for all $a \in A$ and $f(g(b)) = b$ for all $b \in B$.

(Such a function $g$ is called the inverse function of $f$ and is denoted $g = f^{-1}$.)

  1. Prove the "forward direction" of the proposition by assuming that $f \colon A \to B$ is surjective and injective, and concluding that there exists an inverse function.

  2. Prove the "backward direction" of the proposition by assuming that $f^{-1} \colon B \to A$ is an inverse function of $f$, and concluding that $f$ is surjective and injective.

  3. In this part, you will explore why we need to assume that both $g(f(a)) = a$ for all $a \in A$ and $f(g(b)) = b$ for all $b \in B$ in order to conclude that $f$ is a bijection.

    1. Give an example of (nonempty) sets $A$ and $B$ together with a pair of functions $f\colon A \to B$ and $g\colon B \to A$ where
      1. $g(f(a)) = a$ for all $a \in A$,
      2. there exists $b \in B$ such that $f(g(b)) \neq b$, and
      3. $f$ is not a bijection.
    2. Give an example of (nonempty) sets $A$ and $B$ together with a pair of functions $f\colon A \to B$ and $g\colon B \to A$ where
      1. there exists $a \in A$ such that $g(f(a)) \neq a$,
      2. $f(g(b)) = b$ for all $b \in B$, and
      3. $f$ is not a bijection.
    (Hint: for part 3, you may find it easiest to construct these examples using finite sets with 1, 2, or 3 elements.)

Required Exercise 3 [3]

  1. Give an example of a bijection $h: \mathbb{N}_{>0} \to \mathbb{N}_{\geq0}$. Note the domain and the codomain are different, and explain why the map $h(x) = x$ is not a bijection.
  2. Consider the function $f\colon \mathbb{N}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{Z}$ where \[f(n) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\frac{n}{2} & n\ \text{is even}\\[10pt] \displaystyle\frac{-(n+1)}{2} & n\ \text{is odd.}\end{cases}\]
    1. Prove that $f$ is a bijection by proving that it is both injective and surjective.
    2. Prove that $f$ is a bijection by describing a (piecewise-defined) function for the inverse map $g \colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{N}_{\geq 0}$, and checking that $g \circ f\colon \mathbb{N}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{N}_{\geq 0}$ and $f \circ g\colon\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Z}$ are both the identity function on their respective domains.
  3. Describe a bijection $s\colon \mathbb{N}_{>0} \to \mathbb{Z}$ in terms of $h$ and $f$.

Choice Exercise 4 [≤5]

Do any subset of the following parts. For each part that you do, do all subparts.

  1. Show that the map $f \colon \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = x/\pi + \sqrt 2$ is both injective and surjective.
  2. Let $g \colon \mathbb{Z}_{99} \to \mathbb{Z}_{99}$ be defined by $g(n) = n + 19$. Describe an inverse function $g^{-1} \colon \mathbb{Z}_{99} \to \mathbb{Z}_{99}$ and prove that $g(g^{-1}(n)) = g^{-1}(g(n)) = n$ for all $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{99}$.
  3. Let $h \colon \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined by $h(x) = x^2$.
    1. Give an example to prove that $h(x)$ is not injective, and thus is not a bijection.
    2. Why does $k(x) = \sqrt{x}$ fail to be an inverse function for $h$?
  4. [2] Let $f_A \colon A \to A$ be defined by $f_A(x) = x + 1$. Explain your reasoning for each of the following:
    1. Suppose $A = \mathbb{N}$ is the set of nonnegative integers. Is $f_A$ a function from $A \to A$? Is it a bijection?
    2. Suppose $A = \mathcal{E} = \{2n \mid n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is the set of even integers. Is $f_A$ a function from $A \to A$? Is it a bijection?
    3. Suppose $A = \mathbb{Q}$ is the set of rational numbers. Is $f_A$ a function from $A \to A$? Is it a bijection?
    4. Suppose $A = \{n/2 \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ is the set of integers and half-integers. Is $f_A$ a function from $A \to A$? Is it a bijection?

Choice Exercise 5 [≤8]

Do any of the following combinatorics problems about functions.

  1. [2] Suppose that $A$ and $B$ are finite sets and $|A| = |B|$. Prove that a function $f \colon A \to B$ is injective if and only if it is surjective.
  2. [2] Suppose that $A$ and $B$ are finite sets where $|A| = n$ and $|B| = m$. Determine the number of functions $f \colon A \to B$.
  3. [2] Let $A$ be a set, let $F_A = \left\{f\colon A \to \{0,1\}\right\}$ be the set of functions from $A$ to $\{0,1\}$, and let $\mathcal{P}(A)$ be the set of subsets of $A$. Give a bijection $g\colon F_A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$.
  4. [2] Suppose that $A$ and $B$ are finite sets where $|A| = n$ and $|B| = m \geq n$. Determine the number of injections $f \colon A \to B$, and use this to determine the number of bijections between $A$ and $B$ when $m = n$. (In abstract algebra, we call this the symmetric group $S_n$.)

Choice Exercise 6 [5]

In this exercise, you will prove that the relation "there exists a bijection between" is an equivalence relation on sets.

  1. Prove that the relation is reflexive: for all sets $A$, there exists a bijection $f\colon A \to A$.
  2. Prove that the relation is symmetric: for all sets $A$ and $B$ such that there exists a bijection $f\colon A \to B$, there also exists a bijection $g \colon B \to A$.
  3. Prove that the relation is transitive: for all sets $A$, $B$, and $C$ such that there exist bijections $f_1\colon A \to B$ and $f_2\colon B \to C$, there also exists a bijection $f_3 \colon A \to C$.

Choice Exercise 9 [5 + 5]

Do any of the following parts.

  1. [5] Let $\mathcal{A}$ be the set of algebraic numbers, which are complex numbers that are the roots of polynomials, that is, they satisfy $$a_nx^n + a_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \dots + a_2x^2 + a_1x + a_0 = 0$$ for some choice of $n \in \mathbb{N}_{>0}$ and $a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \in \mathbb{Z}$.
