Required Exercises.

Do all 10 points of the following required exercises.

Required Exercise [1 pt]

We're doing a lot of modular arithmetic, you should know how to typeset it.

  1. Type \(726 \equiv 23 \pmod{19}\) to get $726 \equiv 23 \pmod{19}$.
  2. Prove or disprove that $726 \equiv 23 \pmod{19}$.

Required Exercise 2 [3 pts]

Suppose $a \equiv a' \pmod m$ and $b \equiv b' \pmod m$. Prove the following:

  1. $a + b \equiv a' + b' \pmod m$.
  2. $a - b \equiv a' - b' \pmod m$.
  3. $a \times b \equiv a' \times b' \pmod m$.

Choice Exercise 5 [3 pts]

In complex analysis, we define $i$ as the "imaginary unit" so that $i^2 = -1$, and thus $i^3 = -i$, and so on.

  1. We know that $$ i^0 = 1, \qquad i^1 = i, \qquad i^2 = -1, \quad \text{and} \quad i^3 = -i. $$ Prove (by repeated multiplication) that $$ i^4 = 1, \qquad i^5 = i, \qquad i^6 = -1, \quad \text{and} \quad i^7 = -i. $$
  2. Prove that if $n$ and $m$ are positive integers and $n \equiv m \pmod 4$, then $i^n = i^m$.
    (Hint: choose $n \geq m$. Why can you do this?)
  3. Compute $i^{919}$.

Choice Exercise 6 [4 pts]

Andy taught me a cool geometric multiplication trick:

  • Draw the following and take a picture and upload it:
    • Draw the $x$- and $y$-axes and the parabola $y=f(x)=x^2$ for $-2\leq x\leq2$,
    • Choose two real numbers $-2 \leq x_0,x_1 \leq 2$, such as $x_0 = 1.2$ and $x_1 = 1.9$.

  • Does your $y$-intercept equal the product $x_0x_1$? It should! Prove why this works!

Choice Exercise 7 [≤7 points]

Do any subset of the following, up to a maximum of six points.

Prove the following divisibility tricks that you may have learned back when you were first learning arithmetic.

  1. [1 pt] Many of these tricks depend on the facts like $10^n \equiv 1 \pmod 9$ for all integers $n \ge 0$.
  2. [2 pts] Let $n$ be an integer and let $s_n$ be the sum of its digits. For example, if $n = 7261992$ then $s_n = 7+2+6+1+9+9+2=36$, and note that $$ 7261992 = 7 \times 10^6 + 2 \times 10^5 + \dots + 9 \times 10^1 + 2 \times 10^0. $$ Prove that if $3 \mid s_n$, then $3 \mid n$.
  3. [2 pts] Prove the other direction: if $3 \mid n$ then $3 \mid s_n$.
  4. [2 pts] Let $a_n$ be the alternating sum of the digits of $n$. For example, if $n = 7261992$ then $$a_n = 7-2+6-1+9-9+2=12.$$ Prove that if $11 \mid a_n$ then $11 \mid n$.
  5. [2 pts] Prove that if $11 \mid n$ then $11 \mid a_n$.
  6. [3 pts] Let $d_n$ be the number given by the last two digits of $n$. For example, if $n = 7261992$ then $d_n=92$.
    Prove that if $4 \mid n$ then $4 \mid d_n$ and prove that if $4 \mid d_n$ then $4 \mid n$
  7. [3 pts] Let $T_n$ be the number given by "tripling" $n$, so if $m = 19$ then $T_m=191919$.
    Prove that if the number of digits in $n$ is NOT a multiple of $3$, then $37 \mid T_n$.
  8. [3 pts] Prove that if the number of digits in $n$ is a multiple of $3$, then $37 \nmid T_n$.
  9. [4 pts] Take the last digit of $n$, double it, and subtract it from the initial digits. Prove that if this is divisible by $7$, then $n$ is divisible by $7$. (For example, if $n = 259$, you would do $25-18=7$, and thus $7 \mid n$.)

Choice Exercise 8 [6 points]
