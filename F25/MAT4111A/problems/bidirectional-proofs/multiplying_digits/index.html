Multiplying digits

Lucía multiplies some positive one-digit numbers (not necessarily distinct) and obtains a number $n$ greater than $10$. Then, she multiplies all the digits of $n$ and obtains an odd number. Find all possible values of the units digit of $n$.

Pan-American Girls MO 2021, Problem 4

Proof

Proof sketch

We claim that the units digit must be $5$, which occurs when $n = 3(5) = 15$.

First, the units digit cannot be even, because otherwise the product of the digits would be even.

Next we assume for the sake of contradiction that the units digit is $\{1, 3, 7, 9\}$. Since all integers with $n$ as a factor end with $5$ or $0$, $5$ does not occur as a one-digit number. Similarly, no even numbers can occur in the product. Thus the one-digit numbers must be in $\{1, 3, 7, 9\}$, and so must be of the form $n = 3^k 7^\ell$, since $9 = 3^2$. We claim that all such numbers have an even value in the tens place (and thus are of the form $20a + b$ where $b \in \\{1, 3, 7, 9\\}$), proceeding by induction on $k + \ell$. When $k + \ell = 1$, $n = 0(10) + 3$ or $n = 0(10) + 7$. Assume that $n = 3^k 7^\ell = 20a + b$ where $b \in \\{1, 3, 7, 9\\}$ for all $k + \ell < N$. Then either $$\begin{align*}3^{k+1} 7^{\ell} &\in \\{20(3a) + 3, 20(3a) + 9, 20(3a+1) + 1, 20(3a+1) + 7\\} \\\\ 3^k 7^{\ell + 1} &\in \\{20(7a) + 1, 20(7a + 1) + 1, 20(7a + 2) + 9, 20(7a + 3) + 3\\}.\end{align*}$$ In either case, the tens place is even, so by the principle of mathematical induction, $n$ cannot be the number Lucía multiplied, a contradiction. Thus the units digit of $n$ must be $5$.
