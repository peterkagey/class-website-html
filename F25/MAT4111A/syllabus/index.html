Course description

Problem solving techniques as encountered in the William Lowell Putnam Exam.

Textbooks and resources

We will use the following online collections of math contest problems

  • The Putnam Archive by Kiran Kedlaya
  • Virginia Tech Regional Math Competition by Peter Linnell
  • Problems & Solutions, International Mathematics Competition
  • US Ersatz Math Olympiad archive by Evan Chen
  • AMC Problems and Solutions, AoPS

If you have more suggestions, please let me know and I'll add them to the list!

Grading

The grade in this course is intended to reflect your learning, growth, and hard work. Spending time working on problems is essentially the only way to learn mathematics deeply. As such, in-class work will constitute 100% of the points in this class. If you cannot attend class, please contact the instructor for an alternative assignment.

Attendance

We will be discussing ideas and working on problems collaboratively in this class, so as such, attendance is required, and will be reflected your course grade. If you are unable to make it to class, you are encouraged to notify the instructor ahead of time to receive an alternative assignment in order to have the absence excused.

Academic Honesty and the Student Conduct Code

My goal for this class is for you to learn and grow, and your academic honesty is a crucial part of ensuring that happens. You are encouraged to collaborate with other students, come to drop-in hours, ask questions in class, and reach out to me via email when you need help understanding course content. When submitting work done in collaboration with others, acknowledge their contributions, and write up the ideas in your own words.

In this course, you are permitted to use tools such as generative AI (e.g. ChatGPT), Question and Answer sites (e.g. MathStackExchange), and other online resources (e.g. Google, GitHub) given that you acknowledge your use of these tools whenever they impact your written work.

If it appears to me that students are using these tools in a way that undermines their learning or engagement, I may ask individual students to stop using these tools.

Care Center

The Care Center is a first-stop location for any student seeking assistance with basic needs and wellbeing services.

The Care Center can help students who need access to Housing Resources, Food Resources, Financial Resources, Clothing Assistance, Advocacy and Support, and Case Management.

Centers for Transformation, Retention, Equity and Empowerment (TREE)

The Centers for Transformation, Retention, Equity and Empowerment create culturally engaging community spaces to validate and elevate our students' identities, lived experiences, and cultural wealth. We use cultural and identity-based retention frameworks to support the empowerment of our students as they actualize their educational, personal, and career goals.

TREE is made up of six unique Cultural and Identity Centers: Asian and Pacific Islander Student Center, Black Resource Center, César E. Chávez Center for Higher Education, Native American Student Center, Pride Center, and Womxn's Resource Center.

The Learning Resource Center

The Learning Resource Center's mission is to promote student success by providing programs and services that advance students' critical thinking, problem solving, and analytical skills.
